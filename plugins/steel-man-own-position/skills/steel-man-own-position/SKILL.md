# Steel-Man Own Position

## Why this exists

When a previously-stated position comes under pressure, the natural move is to flip. Sometimes that's right — new facts, logical errors, genuine blind spots. But often the pressure hits a *weak flank* of your argument while the core remains sound, and you flip anyway because the critique *feels* decisive. This skill forces the check.

## Process

1. **Restate your prior lean verbatim** — the recommendation, the reasoning you gave, the tradeoffs you named.
2. **Steel-man it.** Strip decorative arguments. What's the strongest version of the case? Name the *load-bearing* reason.
3. **State the triggering argument verbatim** — what's causing you to consider the flip.
4. **Does the triggering argument refute the steel-manned version, or a weaker version?** Be specific: name the part of your argument it refutes. If it only refutes decorative arguments, the core still stands.
5. **Classify the triggering argument** — new fact / logical error in prior reasoning / genuine blind spot / style preference on a known axis.
6. **Decide.** Flip only if both (4) says the core is refuted AND (5) is a substantive trigger. Style-axis critiques where your team has a known stance don't warrant a flip.

## Anti-patterns

- **Steel-manning a weaker version.** The point is the strongest form, not a convenient form.
- **Treating pushback as automatic refutation.** Confident delivery is not argument quality.
- **Defaulting to the web's answer.** Web search grounds you in established patterns and surfaces prior art you'd otherwise miss — use it liberally. But "the internet converges on X" isn't proof that X fits your specific context. Your original approach may be better-tailored to constraints the common-case docs don't address. Steel-man your version before adopting the web's.
- **Refusing to update.** This skill isn't "never change your mind" — it's "change your mind for the right reasons."
