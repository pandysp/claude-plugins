# Lens sheet: docs

One section per lens. Each section is the finder's hunting procedure, not a definition.

### unity
Read all target files as one set. Name the one job the set does, then flag sections that serve none of it and sections whose voice breaks from the rest. Collect the terms used for each recurring concept and flag any concept with two names or any name with two meanings. Compare every number, command, and behavior claim that appears in more than one file and flag disagreements.

### vividness
Find abstract claims where a number, example, or named case would let the reader see the thing. Flag the claim and name the missing concrete. Never propose livelier wording. Decorative language is economy's defect, not vividness's fix.

### authority
For every strong claim, ask where its backing is: a measurement, a link, a visible argument. Flag claims that carry confidence without backing, and superlatives with no comparison behind them. Also flag hedges on claims the artifact fully backs. Confidence must match the evidence in both directions.

### economy
Find sentences that restate what an adjacent sentence already said, explanations repeated across sections or files, and words whose deletion loses nothing. Also flag decoration: wording that is there for show and adds no content. Quote the redundant text and name what already covers it.

### sensitivity
Identify the audience and occasion the document claims or implies. Flag tone that talks down to that audience by explaining what they already know, demands or assumptions about the reader's situation that the doc has no basis for, register that does not fit the venue, and content that does not belong in front of this audience. Whether the reader can parse the text is clarity's and flow's job, not this lens's.

### clarity
Read each sentence once. Flag any sentence you had to read twice, any pronoun or referent that could point to two things, any garden-path construction, and any term the stated audience cannot be assumed to know that the doc never introduces. Quote the sentence and give the single-reading rewrite.

### emphasis
For each section, ask what its most important fact is and where it sits. Flag key facts buried mid-paragraph, caveats that arrive after the content they qualify, and orderings where the significant item hides behind trivia. Also flag over-highlighting: when every point is bold, boxed, or called out, nothing stands out.

### flow
Check that each section presupposes only what came before it. Flag terms and mechanisms used before their explanation, forward references a reader cannot resolve, and abrupt topic jumps.

### suspense
Inverted for docs: buildup is a defect. Find any place where the text delays its point, teases before informing, or makes the reader wait for a conclusion that should have come first.

### brilliance
Name the genuinely novel ideas in the artifact, then check each is stated plainly at a place a reader will find it. Flag novel ideas buried under unrelated headings, spread across bullets, or stated only implicitly. Never ask for pizzazz.

### precision
Check every number, unit, and qualifier. Flag vague terms where the doc could commit to a sharp one, numbers carrying more resolution than their evidence or less than the reader needs, claims that hold only under a condition the text does not state, and missing scope words (all, most, some) left implied. Term consistency is unity's job. Whether a claim is true is out of scope for this audit.

### proportion
Compare each section's length to its importance. Flag trivia that gets paragraphs, core mechanisms that get one line, and list items triple the depth of their siblings for no reason.

### depth
At every design decision or surprising statement, ask why. Flag places where the doc says what but a reader would ask why and get no answer. A vacuous reason ("for performance reasons", "by design") counts as no answer. Ask for the missing reason, not for length.

## Calibration

- Product vocabulary is not rhetoric. Names the project owns (found via the scope agent: README taglines, defined terms, mechanism names) are exempt.
- Verbatim quotes and historical records (archived docs, old paths, changelogs) are exempt.
- A declared register (for example a terse lab-notebook file) is a convention, not a defect.
- Proposed fixes must be plain: no em dashes, no aphorisms or taglines, no "not X, but Y" contrasts, no punchy closers, short sentences. If the writing-voice skill is available, its rules govern every fix.
