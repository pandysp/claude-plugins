# Lens sheet: docs

One section per lens. Each section is the finder's hunting procedure, not a definition.

### unity
Read all target files as one set. Collect the terms used for each recurring concept and flag any concept with two names or any name with two meanings. Compare every number, command, and behavior claim that appears in more than one file and flag disagreements.

### vividness
Find abstract claims that a number, example, or named case would make checkable. Flag the claim and name the missing concrete. Never propose livelier wording; decorative language is not the fix.

### authority
For every strong claim, ask where its backing is: a measurement, a link, a visible argument. Flag claims that carry confidence without backing, and superlatives with no comparison behind them.

### economy
Find sentences that restate what an adjacent sentence already said, explanations repeated across sections or files, and words whose deletion loses nothing. Quote the redundant text and name what already covers it.

### sensitivity
Read as a first-time reader. Flag every term used before the doc introduces it, every silently assumed prerequisite, and any jump in assumed expertise between adjacent sections.

### clarity
Read each sentence once. Flag any sentence you had to read twice, any pronoun or referent that could point to two things, and any garden-path construction. Quote the sentence and give the single-reading rewrite.

### emphasis
For each section, ask what its most important fact is and where it sits. Flag key facts buried mid-paragraph, caveats that arrive after the content they qualify, and orderings where the significant item hides behind trivia.

### flow
Check that each section presupposes only what came before it. Flag terms and mechanisms used before their explanation, forward references a reader cannot resolve, and abrupt topic jumps.

### suspense
Inverted for docs: buildup is a defect. Find any place where the text delays its point, teases before informing, or makes the reader wait for a conclusion that should have come first.

### brilliance
Name the genuinely novel ideas in the artifact, then check each is stated plainly at a place a reader will find it. Flag novel ideas buried under unrelated headings, spread across bullets, or stated only implicitly. Never ask for pizzazz.

### precision
Check every number, unit, and qualifier. Flag claims that are approximately wrong, unqualified where a condition applies, or inconsistent with a measurement elsewhere in the artifact. Flag terminology that drifts between sections.

### proportion
Compare each section's length to its importance. Flag trivia that gets paragraphs, core mechanisms that get one line, and list items triple the depth of their siblings for no reason.

### depth
At every design decision or surprising statement, ask why. Flag places where the doc says what but a reader would ask why and get no answer. Ask for the missing reason, not for length.

## Calibration

- Product vocabulary is not rhetoric. Names the project owns (found via the scope agent: README taglines, defined terms, mechanism names) are exempt.
- Verbatim quotes and historical records (archived docs, old paths, changelogs) are exempt.
- A declared register (for example a terse lab-notebook file) is a convention, not a defect.
- Proposed fixes must be plain: no em dashes, no aphorisms or taglines, no "not X, but Y" contrasts, no punchy closers, short sentences. If the writing-voice skill is available, its rules govern every fix.
