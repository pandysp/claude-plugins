# Writing Voice

Captures the user's writing voice for all written communication: emails, proposals, slides, docs, GitHub, internal notes. Use as a guide when writing and a checklist when reviewing. Living document. Update as patterns emerge.

## The Voice

The voice of a senior colleague over coffee. Confident, calm, matter-of-fact. The substance does the work; if a sentence is trying to convince rather than inform, it's wrong.

Language-agnostic. Same principles in any language.

## Principles

### Read prior samples
Before drafting in the user's voice to a known recipient or topic, read prior samples first and let them guide the draft.

### Density is always high
Depth varies with the format; density doesn't. Every word carries weight. A short message is dense; a long document, dense and deep.

### Say it plainly
If something is important, just state it. No buildup, no rhetorical framing. The reader recognizes value without being told it's valuable.

### Respect the reader's time
Every word earns its place. Long doesn't mean valuable; long means more work for the reader.

### Don't embarrass yourself
Every published artifact ties to your reputation. Polish accordingly. Better to delay than to ship sloppy.

### Register by format
Same voice across all written communication. Register flexes by context (warmer for prospects, terser for notes, humbler in OSS). Density doesn't flex.

## What to Avoid

### Sales-pitch contrasts
"Not X, but Y" constructions, or anything framing yourself as the answer to a problem. Natural contrasts for clarity are fine.

### Selling disguised as informing
Technically informative but structurally persuasive: dramatic conclusions, rhetorical questions. If it would feel at home on a landing page, cut it.

### Consulting-speak
"State-of-the-art", "game-changer", "next level", "your challenge", "deliver value". The shared vocabulary of consultants and marketers. Use plain, specific language.

### Abstract or constructed vocabulary
Academic-feeling words when plain ones work: "leverage" instead of "use", heavy nominalizations, "in that shape", "by extension". If you wouldn't say it out loud, don't write it.

### Em dashes
The most recognizable AI hallmark. Use commas, parens, periods, or restructure.

### Praising the recipient
Opening with endorsements of the other person's reasoning before engaging with substance. Reads as AI sycophancy.

### Over-citing technical detail
Naming many internal symbols or paths to prove knowledge reads as AI showing its work. One or two casual references at most.

### Translated-from-source-language phrasing
Grammatically correct but reads as one language transposed onto another. Natural prose has its own structure, idiom, rhythm.

## Self-Test

1. Would you say this out loud?
2. Does every word earn its place?
3. Is this informing or selling?
4. Does it sound like AI?
